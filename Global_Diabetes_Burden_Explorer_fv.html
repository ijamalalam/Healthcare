Findings

Five evidence-based observations drawn from the published estimates above. See About for data sources and limitations.

Finding 01 · high prevalence

Highest age-standardised prevalence in the set

Pakistan exhibited the highest age-standardised diabetes prevalence among the selected countries (31.4% in 2024), substantially exceeding the next-highest estimate (United States, 13.7%) and approximately six-fold the lowest (Austria, 5.4%). The estimate is projected to reach 34.2% by 2050.

Finding 02 · rapid projected increase

Steepest projected growth in absolute burden

The estimated number of adults living with diabetes in Pakistan is projected to rise from 34.5M (2024) to 70.2M (2050) which is a increase of roughly 103%, the largest in the set (India +75%, China +14%, United States +12%; Germany and Austria approximately stable).

Finding 03 · detection gap

Undiagnosed share is wide and not explained by spending

The estimated undiagnosed proportion ranged from 49.7% (China) and 43.0% (India) to 20.4% (Germany). Belgium's comparatively high undiagnosed share (37.0%) despite substantial expenditure indicates that detection reflects health-system factors beyond spending alone.

Finding 04 · expenditure mismatch

Spending runs inverse to burden

Diabetes-related expenditure per person ranged from ≈US$79 (Pakistan) to ≈US$10,497 (United States), inversely related to prevalence and to the diabetes-attributable share of mortality, which was highest in Pakistan (24.8% of deaths in adults 20–79).

Finding 05 · implications for surveillance

Burden concentrates where detection systems are thinnest

Taken together, the concentration of burden in lower-expenditure settings with large undiagnosed fractions underscores the value of standardised registries and routine surveillance for earlier detection and resource prioritisation.

About this project

Exploratory project: what it is, how it was made, and what it does not claim to be.

Purpose

Developed as an independent analytical exercise inspired by the work of the IDF Epidemiology Team, to demonstrate descriptive epidemiological comparison and interactive data communication using publicly available estimates.

Methods
  • Descriptive epidemiological comparison across countries
  • Trend and projection interpretation (2000–2050)
  • Interactive visualisation of published estimates
Data source

IDF Diabetes Atlas, 11th edition (2025), publicly available country-level exports. Age-standardised estimates for adults aged 20–79, reference year 2024 with projections to 2050. Figures were transcribed from the published estimates; the underlying primary data were not independently reprocessed.

Build & tools

This dashboard was built in HTML, JavaScript, and Chart.js as a single self-contained file. Author's wider analytics toolkit: Power BI, Python, R, and SQL.

Limitations

This is an interactive visualisation of published estimates, not an independent reanalysis of primary data. Comparisons cover seven selected countries only and are not globally representative. All figures are published, age-standardised estimates; projections to 2050 depend on the Atlas's underlying modelling assumptions, and some year-to-year shifts reflect changes in national data sources between Atlas editions rather than real-world reversals. The project is not affiliated with or endorsed by the International Diabetes Federation.
